Problem Statement

Current Topic

Smart Safety Management for Tourists Traveling in North East India

This problem statement focuses on the need for a government-backed digital safety platform for tourism in geographically sensitive and low-connectivity regions.

Background

North East India is one of the most attractive tourism regions in the country because of its mountains, forests, waterfalls, wildlife, festivals, tribal culture, and adventure destinations. Every year, tourists travel to the region for eco-tourism, spiritual tourism, cultural tourism, and outdoor experiences. Although the region has strong tourism potential, tourist movement is often affected by harsh terrain, changing climate conditions, remote travel corridors, weak mobile connectivity, and limited access to trusted local support services.

Existing Problem

Tourists may unknowingly enter unsafe areas, book unverified local services, lose route clarity in remote places, or face delays during emergencies because available systems are fragmented. A map application may show roads, a booking platform may show hotels, and an SOS tool may share location, but these systems do not work together as one tourism safety mechanism. They also do not give government authorities a live and structured view of tourist safety conditions across sensitive zones.

Need for the System

A dedicated system is needed to continuously support tourists before and during travel. The system should recommend verified accommodation, transport, guides, and event places; identify dynamic safety zones; detect risky movement through geofencing; send timely warnings; and allow tourists to trigger instant panic alerts with live location sharing. At the same time, it should help the Department of Tourism, police, and rescue teams monitor incidents and coordinate responses more effectively.

Final Problem Statement

The major problem is that North East India lacks an integrated, government-supported tourist protection platform that can combine live location monitoring, AI-based safe recommendations, geofencing alerts, verified tourism services, and emergency response coordination in one system. As a result, tourists may experience safety risks, lower travel confidence, and delayed support in critical situations. Therefore, a Smart Tourist Protection System is required to improve tourist safety, strengthen administrative visibility, and promote secure and sustainable tourism development in the region.

SDLC Model

Chosen Development Model

Spiral Model for a Risk-Sensitive Government Safety System

The project involves emergency workflows, AI-based decisions, live tracking, and public-sector supervision, so the development process must support both iteration and risk control.

Why Spiral Model Is Suitable

The Spiral Model is most suitable because this project cannot be built safely in one straight sequence. The system includes changing safety rules, real-time alert mechanisms, admin intervention, geofencing, AI-assisted recommendations, and location privacy concerns. Each iteration of the spiral allows the team to gather feedback, analyze risks, build a controlled version of the system, and improve it in the next cycle. This is especially important for a government-supported platform where accuracy, reliability, and safety are critical.

Model Selection Justification

  • Requirements may evolve after pilot deployment in different states or tourist zones.
  • Emergency and geofencing features require repeated validation before wide release.
  • AI recommendations must be tested and refined under admin supervision.
  • Connectivity challenges and field conditions create technical and operational risks.
  • The system benefits from phased release rather than one-time delivery.

SRS

Formal Documentation Section

Software Requirements Specification

This section defines the software requirements of the Smart Tourist Protection System in a structured form, including purpose, scope, users, functional requirements, and non-functional requirements.

1. Purpose

The purpose of this system is to provide a government-supported digital safety platform for tourists traveling in North East India. The software helps users discover verified travel services, receive risk alerts, share live location during emergencies, and obtain safer travel guidance through intelligent recommendations and administrative monitoring.

2. Scope

The system consists of an Android application for tourists and a web-based portal for the Department of Tourism and authorized officials. It supports maps, safety zones, emergency alerts, service verification, AI-assisted recommendations, multilingual access, and optional IoT integration for high-risk zones.

Product Name Government-Supported Smart Tourist Protection System for North East India
Primary Users Tourists, Admins, Service Providers, Police, Rescue Teams
Deployment Android App, Web Portal, Cloud Backend

3. Functional Requirements

  • FR1: Tourists shall be able to register, log in, and manage profile information.
  • FR2: The application shall display live tourist location on a map.
  • FR3: The system shall show red, orange, and yellow safety zones.
  • FR4: The system shall provide AI-assisted recommendations for attractions, events, hotels, transport, and guides.
  • FR5: Admins shall review and supervise recommendation outputs and service verification.
  • FR6: Geofencing shall detect tourist entry into restricted or high-risk zones.
  • FR7: The application shall send alerts to tourists and authorities when risk conditions occur.
  • FR8: A panic button shall send distress alerts with live location.
  • FR9: Optional live tracking shall be available for family members and law enforcement.
  • FR10: The system shall calculate a tourist safety score using movement pattern and area sensitivity.
  • FR11: Local providers shall be able to submit hotels, homestays, transport, guides, and events for approval.
  • FR12: Optional IoT bands or tags shall be supported for high-risk travel zones.

4. Non-Functional Requirements

  • NFR1: The system should process alerts with near real-time response.
  • NFR2: The platform should support many concurrent users during festivals and peak seasons.
  • NFR3: The mobile app should remain usable under unstable or low-bandwidth connectivity.
  • NFR4: The system should use lightweight communication for maps, tracking, and alerts.
  • NFR5: Sensitive user and location data should be protected through secure access control and encryption.
  • NFR6: The platform should provide audit logs for administrative and emergency operations.
  • NFR7: The portal should be scalable, reliable, and available for continuous monitoring.
  • NFR8: The application should support multilingual access for broader usability.

5. Major User Classes

Tourists Tourism Department Authorized Admins Service Providers Police / Rescue Teams Maintainers

6. Summary

The SRS shows that the system is not only a tourism information application but a safety-oriented digital platform that combines verified services, government supervision, AI support, emergency response, and risk monitoring in a single integrated solution.

Class Diagram

1. Class, Attribute, and Method Explanation

  • Tourist: This class stores the tourist's identity and safety information. `touristId` identifies the tourist, `name` stores the tourist name, `language` stores the selected language, and `safetyScore` stores the calculated safety level. The method `triggerPanic()` is used to activate an emergency alert.
  • LocationUpdate: This class stores movement data sent from the tourist's device. `locationId` identifies each location record, `latitude` and `longitude` store the coordinates, and `timestamp` stores the time of update. The method `sendToServer()` sends this data to the backend.
  • SafetyZone: This class stores zone-based safety information. `zoneId` identifies the zone, `zoneType` stores the zone category, `riskLevel` stores the danger level, and `geofenceBoundary` stores the area boundary. The method `evaluateRisk()` checks whether the tourist is in a risky zone.
  • Alert: This class stores warning or emergency information. `alertId` identifies the alert, `alertType` stores the type of alert, `status` stores its current state, and `createdAt` stores the time of generation. The method `dispatch()` sends the alert forward.
  • ServiceProvider: This class stores local tourism service details. `providerId` identifies the provider, `serviceType` stores the category of service, `verificationStatus` stores approval state, and `contactInfo` stores communication details. The method `submitService()` is used to register or send service information.
  • RecommendationEngine: This class handles recommendation logic. `modelVersion` stores the algorithm version, `ruleSet` stores recommendation rules, and `confidenceScore` stores ranking confidence. The methods `rankServices()` and `suggestZones()` support safer recommendations.
  • Admin: This class represents the supervisory authority. `adminId` identifies the admin user, `role` stores the authority level, and `department` stores the administrative unit. The methods `approveService()` and `updateZone()` control service approval and safety-zone management.
  • IoTDevice: This class represents a smart band or tracking tag. `deviceId` identifies the device, `deviceType` stores its type, and `pairingStatus` stores whether it is connected to a tourist. The method `syncSignal()` sends device-related safety data.

2. Interworking of Classes

  • The Tourist class sends travel data through LocationUpdate.
  • LocationUpdate is checked against SafetyZone to detect travel risk.
  • If a risky condition is found, the system creates an Alert.
  • The Tourist may use services registered under ServiceProvider.
  • RecommendationEngine helps the tourist by ranking safer and more suitable service options.
  • Admin reviews recommendations, verifies services, and manages safety zones.
  • IoTDevice can also send distress-related signals that support alert generation.
  • Together, all classes cooperate to form a smart tourist safety and emergency response system.

3. Overall Working of the Model

  • The model is centered around the tourist and the tourist's movement.
  • The system constantly receives live travel information and compares it with predefined safety zones.
  • When danger is detected, the system generates alerts for warning and emergency response.
  • Alongside protection, the system also supports safer tourism choices through verified services and recommendations.
  • Administrative monitoring and IoT support strengthen the complete safety framework.

Use Case Diagram

UML Behavioral Model

Use Case Diagram Based on Tourist Safety System Functions

This use case diagram shows how the main external actors interact with the Smart Tourist Protection System through the key services suggested by the class diagram and overall project requirements.

1. Main Actors and Their Use Cases

  • Tourist: The tourist can register, share live location, view verified services, receive zone warnings, get safe recommendations, and trigger panic alerts.
  • Admin: The admin verifies services, reviews recommendation outputs, and manages safety zones and alerts.
  • Service Provider: The service provider submits hotels, guides, transport, events, or homestay information into the system.
  • Police / Rescue: Emergency responders interact with the panic alert and alert-management process during risky situations.

2. Relation with the Class Diagram

  • The tourist use cases relate to the Tourist and LocationUpdate classes.
  • Zone warning and panic handling relate to SafetyZone and Alert.
  • Service submission and verification relate to ServiceProvider and Admin.
  • Safe recommendation functions relate to RecommendationEngine.
  • Emergency-support interaction is also consistent with the IoTDevice and alert flow shown in the class diagram.

Use Case 1. Login

  • 1.1 Introduction: This use case describes how an authorized user logs into the Smart Tourist Protection System.
  • 1.2 Actors: Tourist, Admin, Service Provider, Police/Rescue.
  • 1.3 Pre-Conditions: None.
  • 1.4 Post-Conditions: If successful, the actor is logged into the system. Otherwise, the system state remains unchanged.
  • 1.5 Basic Flow: The system asks the actor to enter user ID and password. The actor enters the credentials. The system validates them and grants access if they are correct.
  • 1.6 Alternate Flow: If invalid credentials are entered, the system displays an error message and asks the actor to retry or cancel.
  • 1.7 Special Requirements: Secure password handling and role-based access control.
  • 1.8 Use Case Relationships: This use case is a precondition for admin, provider, and responder-specific operations.

Use Case 2. Track Location

  • 2.1 Introduction: This use case allows the system to receive and process live tourist location data.
  • 2.2 Actors: Tourist.
  • 2.3 Pre-Conditions: Tourist must be logged in and location permission must be enabled.
  • 2.4 Post-Conditions: Tourist location is stored or updated in the system for monitoring and safety evaluation.
  • 2.5 Basic Flow: The tourist opens the app and allows location access. The app captures coordinates and sends them to the backend. The system stores the update and checks it against active safety zones.
  • 2.6 Alternate Flow: If location permission is denied or the network is unavailable, the system stores pending data locally or asks the tourist to enable location services.
  • 2.7 Special Requirements: Low-data communication and support for unstable connectivity.
  • 2.8 Use Case Relationships: Supports Receive Warnings, Trigger Panic, and Manage Zones & Alerts.

Use Case 3. Receive Warnings

  • 3.1 Introduction: This use case allows the tourist to receive warnings when approaching or entering unsafe zones.
  • 3.2 Actors: Tourist.
  • 3.3 Pre-Conditions: Tourist location must be available and safety-zone data must exist in the system.
  • 3.4 Post-Conditions: A warning is shown to the tourist and the event may be logged for monitoring.
  • 3.5 Basic Flow: The system compares live tourist coordinates with zone boundaries. If the tourist reaches a warning or danger zone, the system generates a safety alert and sends a notification.
  • 3.6 Alternate Flow: If no danger zone is detected, no warning is sent and monitoring continues.
  • 3.7 Special Requirements: Near real-time notification delivery.
  • 3.8 Use Case Relationships: Depends on Track Location and Safety Zone Management.

Use Case 4. Trigger Panic

  • 4.1 Introduction: This use case allows the tourist to send an emergency alert with live location.
  • 4.2 Actors: Tourist, Police/Rescue, Admin.
  • 4.3 Pre-Conditions: Tourist must be logged in and panic service must be available.
  • 4.4 Post-Conditions: A panic alert is created and sent to the relevant authority and optionally family members.
  • 4.5 Basic Flow: The tourist taps the panic button. The app sends the current location and user details to the server. The system creates a panic alert and notifies emergency responders and the admin dashboard.
  • 4.6 Alternate Flow: If the network is weak, the system retries transmission or falls back to the available emergency channel.
  • 4.7 Special Requirements: Fast response and high reliability.
  • 4.8 Use Case Relationships: Uses Track Location and Handle Emergency.

Use Case 5. Submit Services

  • 5.1 Introduction: This use case allows a service provider to submit tourism-related service details.
  • 5.2 Actors: Service Provider.
  • 5.3 Pre-Conditions: Service provider must be logged in.
  • 5.4 Post-Conditions: Service information is stored in pending or submitted state for review.
  • 5.5 Basic Flow: The service provider selects the service submission option, enters service details such as category, contact information, and availability, and submits the form. The system stores the record and marks it for verification.
  • 5.6 Alternate Flow: If required data is missing, the system displays an error and asks the provider to correct the form.
  • 5.7 Special Requirements: Validation of mandatory fields and duplicate checks.
  • 5.8 Use Case Relationships: Followed by Verify Services.

Use Case 6. Verify Services

  • 6.1 Introduction: This use case allows the admin to verify, approve, or reject submitted tourism service listings.
  • 6.2 Actors: Admin.
  • 6.3 Pre-Conditions: Admin must be logged in and submitted service records must exist.
  • 6.4 Post-Conditions: The service is approved, rejected, or updated in the system.
  • 6.5 Basic Flow: The admin opens pending service records, reviews the details, checks authenticity, and selects approve or reject. The system updates the verification status.
  • 6.6 Alternate Flow: If the record is incomplete or suspicious, the admin may keep it pending or reject it.
  • 6.7 Special Requirements: Audit trail for verification actions.
  • 6.8 Use Case Relationships: Related to Submit Services and View Services.

Use Case 7. Manage Zones & Alerts

  • 7.1 Introduction: This use case allows the admin to manage safety zones and review alert conditions.
  • 7.2 Actors: Admin.
  • 7.3 Pre-Conditions: Admin must be logged in.
  • 7.4 Post-Conditions: Safety zones and alert statuses are updated in the system.
  • 7.5 Basic Flow: The admin opens the monitoring dashboard, reviews zone status, changes zone categories if required, and manages active alerts or incidents. The system updates the affected records.
  • 7.6 Alternate Flow: If no alerts or updates are required, the system remains unchanged.
  • 7.7 Special Requirements: Fast dashboard response and role-based control.
  • 7.8 Use Case Relationships: Supports Receive Warnings and Trigger Panic.

Use Case 8. Handle Emergency

  • 8.1 Introduction: This use case allows police or rescue teams to respond to panic alerts and dangerous incidents.
  • 8.2 Actors: Police/Rescue.
  • 8.3 Pre-Conditions: An active panic alert or incident must exist.
  • 8.4 Post-Conditions: The incident is acknowledged, updated, or resolved in the system.
  • 8.5 Basic Flow: The responder receives the panic alert, checks location and incident details, initiates the response, and updates the response status in the system.
  • 8.6 Alternate Flow: If the alert is invalid or already resolved, the responder records the status and closes the event.
  • 8.7 Special Requirements: Reliable alert visibility and responder coordination.
  • 8.8 Use Case Relationships: Triggered by Trigger Panic and related to Manage Zones & Alerts.

Entity Details

  • Tourist provides Service Provider shows that tourists interact with listed tourism services available in the system.
  • Admin verifies Service Provider ensures that only approved local services remain trusted and visible.
  • Police Rescue handles Alert represents emergency intervention after warning or panic situations are created.
  • Police Rescue updates Safety Zone reflects field-based safety feedback and operational zone changes.

Sequence Diagram

Scenario: Tourist Enters a High-Risk Zone

What the Sequence Diagram Shows

This sequence diagram shows the step-by-step interaction that occurs when a tourist moves into a high-risk zone. The Tourist App first sends the live location to the Backend, the Backend asks the Geofence Engine to evaluate the area, and the returned risk status is used to create a risk alert. The Alert Service then pushes a warning back to the Tourist App, publishes the incident to the Admin Portal, receives acknowledgement, and finally sends a safe route suggestion to help the tourist move toward a safer area.

Message Flow Summary

  • 1. Tourist App -> Backend: sendLocation()
  • 2. Backend -> Geofence Engine: checkZone()
  • 3. Geofence Engine -> Backend: riskStatus = RED
  • 4. Backend -> Alert Service: createRiskAlert()
  • 5. Alert Service -> Tourist App: pushWarning()
  • 6. Alert Service -> Admin Portal: publishIncident()
  • 7. Admin Portal -> Alert Service: acknowledge()
  • 8. Alert Service -> Tourist App: sendSafeRouteSuggestion()

Interaction Diagram

Interaction Overview: Panic Alert Coordination

What the Interaction Diagram Shows

This interaction overview diagram presents the control flow of the tourist emergency handling workflow. It starts from the initial node, checks whether the tourist request is accepted or rejected, and then moves through smaller interaction frames that capture risk data, check the zone, notify the tourist, and dispatch emergency response support. The fork and join nodes show how multiple interaction paths are coordinated before reaching the final activity end.

Main Interaction Flow

  • Initial node starts the emergency interaction control flow.
  • Decision node checks whether the tourist request is valid and safe to continue.
  • ref: validate tourist status represents checking the tourist state before processing.
  • ref: reject unsafe request represents the alternate rejection path.
  • Fork node splits the accepted control flow into parallel interaction frames.
  • sd Capture Risk captures live location information from the tourist and forwards it to the backend.
  • sd Check Zone checks the incoming data against geofence and risk-zone logic.
  • Join node synchronizes both interaction paths before the next decision.
  • sd Notify Tourist sends the generated warning back to the tourist side.
  • sd Dispatch Response sends the alert to rescue authorities for action.
  • Activity final marks the completion of the interaction workflow.
